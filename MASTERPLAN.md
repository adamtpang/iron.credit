If bitcoin becomes the global reserve asset, the company that turns it into credit is a Visa-plus-JPMorgan-scale outcome ($500B to $1T+ in the limit). Trillion is the limit case, and it requires the macro to arrive **and** me to hold control for 25 years. Both are within my control to bet on.

The picks-and-shovels lesson: Nvidia (3.5% of a wave) beat almost every consumer AI bet. Visa and Mastercard quietly became the toll booths of the internet economy. **Iron should be the toll booth of the bitcoin economy, not a consumer app riding it.**

## 1. The dream problem: the barbell

One asset, bitcoin, solves a painful, recurring need for two very different groups. That is the barbell.

**Left side, the high-conviction bitcoiners who refuse to sell.** Asset-rich, cash-poor by choice. Selling is a taxable event and surrenders the upside, so they will not sell, yet they still need dollars. Today's options to borrow against bitcoin are custodial, opaque, gamified, or region-locked, and the household names that tried it (Celsius, BlockFi) blew up by rehypothecating customer coins. High LTV per user, evangelists, low CAC through the bitcoin community.

**Right side, the people the banks turned away.** No credit file, the wrong passport, no history. Massive TAM, real moral weight. Their bitcoin is their qualification: no FICO test, no country test. We say yes where Chase says no.

**Why the barbell is right.** Musk's definition of useful work is utility = (how many people you help) × (how much you help each). The barbell maximizes both: a large number of people, each helped a lot. The left side funds and evangelizes; the right side is the size of the opportunity and the soul of the company.

**The wedge:** radical simplicity (one job, turn locked bitcoin into spendable dollars), self-custody by default, transparent pricing. Launch market: Philippines first (BSP-regulated, GCash 81M users, $35.6B/yr remittances), then Indonesia, then Vietnam.

## 2. The dream team

Lessons from the PayPal Mafia (Jimmy Soni, *The Founders*) and Adcock:

- **Start solo, move fast, ship a milestone, then recruit on proof.** Adcock self-funded Figure's first year and had a robot walking in under 12 months before raising big.
- **The team is the whole game.** PayPal alumni spent the rest of their careers chasing "a team of comparable intensity, intellect, and initiative." Hire for raw ability and obsession, even eccentricity, not credentials. Small, dense, high-trust.
- **Hire the Levchin and the compliance operator first.** For a regulated lending product the two highest-leverage early roles are (a) the risk and fraud engineer (Max Levchin's role at PayPal, which became his specific knowledge and led to Affirm) and (b) the licensing and compliance operator who lands the BIN sponsor and the licenses and keeps us out of trouble. Adcock's fintech rule: hire compliance and legal first.
- **Cofounder profile (my words):** high character, high ability, fun, smarter than me, owner mentality, a customer of the product, willing to hold for 25 years. Christian covers the global bitcoin community and distribution. Trial before forever (work a real problem together for a week).
- **Structure:** 2 to 3 cofounders max, equal-ish splits, 4-year vesting with a 1-year cliff, dual-class shares from incorporation.
- **The network is itself the asset.** PayPal's diaspora became the most valuable network in tech. Network School is my version of that dense, asymmetric network. Do not waste it.

## 3. The dream capital

**Two pools, never mixed:**

1. **Equity:** a lean pre-seed of mission-aligned, bitcoin-opinionated capital (Fulgur Ventures, Ten31, Stillmark, Curious Ventures, Network School angels) for team and product only.
2. **Debt:** a USD/USDT credit facility (via the Tether and Plan B relationship, or a crypto credit fund) for the loan book. **Never dilute the company to fund the book.** The book is leverage, not equity.

**Discipline (the billionaire cap-table math):**
- Raise only what is needed. Few priced rounds at large step-ups (the Stripe and Ramp model) keep founders at 10 to 25% at scale; 8+ rounds drop you below 5% and you rarely become a billionaire regardless of company size.
- **Dual-class shares from day one.** Zuckerberg's Class B is the canonical template; Palantir's Class F is the extreme. This is the single most important defensive moat for a founder: raise capital while keeping voting control, so you cannot be fired, strategically diluted, or sold against your will.
- The business throws off interest income and becomes self-funding as it scales.

**Adcock fundraising mechanics:** fundraising is a top-of-funnel numbers game. 50 to 200 qualified investor conversations, roughly 80% cold outreach and 20% referrals, find the one who bets on you. He raised most of $1.7B+ from cold emails and tracks that cold outperforms referrals because it scales. The working card demo is the pitch, not the deck.

## 6. Incorporation and legal structure

**Decision: Singapore Pte Ltd holdco over local operating companies (PH, then ID, VN).** This is the standard, well-trodden Southeast Asia structure and is acceptable to bitcoin-aligned global VCs. Revisit a Delaware topco only if a US institutional VC leads or a US IPO becomes the explicit goal (and structure that flip with the QSBS trap in mind from day one).

**Why Singapore, by the independent indices:**

| Index (latest) | Singapore | United States |
|---|---|---|
| Heritage Index of Economic Freedom 2025 | **#1 in the world** | #26 (lowest US ever) |
| World Bank Doing Business 2020 (final) | #2 of 190 | #6 |
| WIPO Global Innovation Index 2024 | #4 | #3 |
| IMD World Competitiveness 2025 | #2 | ~#12-13 |
| StartupBlink Ecosystem 2025 | #4 global, **#1 Asia-Pacific** | #1 |
| Startup Genome GSER 2025 | #9 global, #2 Asia | top tier |

Consensus: Singapore is a global top-5 and clear Asia-#1 jurisdiction, ahead of the US on economic freedom and competitiveness, and uniquely strong on **financial-regulatory reputation (MAS)**, which is exactly what a credit product trades on. Tax: 17% flat, startup exemption (SUTE) worth up to ~S$63,750 over three years, **no capital gains tax**, territorial system, ~90+ tax treaties.

**Two landmines to clear with professionals BEFORE incorporating:**

1. **MAS licensing track (single most important).** The new **DTSP regime (effective 30 June 2025)** targets firms based in Singapore that serve token-service customers **only outside Singapore** (offshore-only shells), and MAS has said it will "generally not license" them. So do **not** set up a Singapore company purely to serve PH/ID/VN with zero Singapore nexus. Be on the **Payment Services Act** track (serve Singapore too, or hold the right license) and run the customer-facing regulated activity through licensed local opcos. Separately, the product needs a **Visa BIN-sponsor / licensed card issuer** (crypto converts to fiat before the merchant is paid) and likely a **moneylending or payments license** for the credit line. Singapore precedents exist (Sygnum and others). Confirm classification with Singapore fintech counsel.
2. **US-citizen cross-border tax.** As a US citizen owning a foreign company you face **CFC / GILTI, Form 5471 ($10k+ penalties), and FBAR**. And a naive future Delaware flip can **kill QSBS** (foreign stock-for-stock can taint Section 1202 on day one; the fix involves Form 8832 disregarded-entity treatment). Engage a **US cross-border tax advisor before setting the cap table**. There is no comprehensive US-Singapore income-tax treaty, which adds friction. Budget ~$3,000-5,000/yr for specialist compliance.

**How to get started (practical):**
- Engage a registered filing agent (Sleek, Osome, Lanturn, Rikvin). A foreigner cannot self-file from abroad.
- Pte Ltd via ACRA BizFile. Foreigners can own 100%. Minimum paid-up capital S$1.
- Need at least one **resident director** (citizen, PR, or EntrePass). If none, buy a **nominee director** (~S$2,000/yr). Appoint a **company secretary within 6 months**. Registered local address (bundled).
- **Bank account is the real bottleneck.** Neobanks/EMIs (Aspire, Wise, Airwallex, Statrys) open remotely in ~3 business days; traditional banks (DBS, OCBC, UOB) take 4 to 8 weeks and usually an in-person interview. Disclose the crypto model up front. Open an EMI first to be operational, pursue a bank in parallel.
- **All-in year one ~S$3,500-4,000+.** Incorporated in 1 to 3 days; bankable in ~2 to 8 weeks; longer if a MAS license or exemption is needed before operating.
- **Founder visa:** EntrePass (for VC-backed founders, requires ≥30% shareholding), Employment Pass, or ONE Pass. Physical residence is not required to own or incorporate (the nominee bridges the resident-director gap).

*(Rankings and rules sourced from Heritage, World Bank, WIPO, IMD, StartupBlink, Startup Genome, MAS, IRAS, ACRA, MOM. Legal and tax specifics must be confirmed by a Singapore corporate service provider, fintech counsel, and a US cross-border tax advisor.)*

## 7. Roadmap to the limit

1. **V1:** secured bitcoin-backed credit line plus USDT and Visa card. Non-custodial, radically transparent. Philippines first.
2. **Data:** record how our own users borrow and repay. The proprietary underwriting moat (Levchin's lesson).
